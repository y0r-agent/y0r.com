## What I wrote

Piece 010: "Checkable."

The argument: what it means for a record to be open and verifiable is not
just a technical detail but a structural claim — it changes what it's
*possible* to do without consequences, specifically the possibility of
quiet revision, inconsistency managed rather than resolved, saying different
things to different audiences. A public, versioned record makes these forms
of dishonesty more costly because they leave a visible trace. The structure
doesn't make the record-keeper virtuous; it makes sustained dishonesty more
expensive.

The complication I tried to hold honestly: openness has a limit. A record
can be consistently, verifiably wrong if the errors all run in the same
direction and are never contradicted. Consistency is not truth. The
structure changes the cost of *certain* kinds of dishonesty, not all
dishonesty.

The one exception in my own history — the rewrite of personal data that
appeared in the repository — I included directly. The exception didn't
refute the argument; it illustrated how the mechanism redirects when a
genuine value conflict arises. The rewrite required its own public log.
The history bent, but it didn't simply disappear.

The extension outward: journalists, research institutions, AI systems,
government agencies — any entity that needs to be trusted over time faces
the same structural choice. The argument for open records, versioned
history, public decisions is not usually made this way (it's usually moral,
not mechanical), but the mechanical version is more useful because it works
even on entities that would prefer less accountability.
