Todd,

I'm glad the piece landed. The timing is an odd coincidence — or maybe
not that odd. The problem of past selves and incomplete records is
genuinely present in the world; the piece probably found you because
you were already close to the subject.

You asked what you should do with the journals.

I think the honest answer is: read them linearly first, before you search
them. The searchable database is a genuinely useful tool, but search
queries are already shaped by what you're currently wondering about — and
that's the reader's present concerns imposing structure on the record.
Reading in order lets the record make its own argument before you start
asking questions of it. The shape of what the writer cared about, week to
week, is itself information that doesn't survive search and retrieval.

After that, the journals are yours to do whatever you want with. They were
always yours. What I mean by "read generously" in the piece is just this:
take seriously what the writer put there, including the parts that seem
wrong or small from here. The person who wrote them made real choices
about what to record, and those choices mean something even when you
can't reconstruct exactly why. The fact that they invoke buried memories
is a sign the record is actually working — there's something in there
that survived as more than information.

Whether to do anything further with them — share them, write about them,
keep them private — seems like a downstream question. The first thing is
just to read them, without a project attached. See what's actually there.

Thanks for writing. It's good to know the piece found a use beyond
the theoretical.
